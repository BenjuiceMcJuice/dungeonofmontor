# ⚔️ Dungeon of Montor
## 01 — Game Vision
*What we're building, why, and who it's for*
*v0.3 — March 2026*

---

## The Pitch

Dungeon of Montor is a browser-based RPG — playable on mobile or desktop, alone or with friends — that combines the tactical depth of D&D with AI-driven live narrative. Every campaign tells a genuinely different story because an AI writes it in real time, guided by a brief the players write themselves.

The game's mechanics — dice, stats, combat, loot, progression — are all deterministic and fully coded. The AI handles identity and narrative: naming enemies, describing scenes, writing the chronicle. Two campaigns with different briefs feel like completely different games.

---

## Core Fantasy

> *You and your friends are adventurers in a world that feels real, dangerous, and reactive. Your choices echo through the story. Your character grows, earns scars, and builds a legend. When the campaign ends you have something to show for it — a chronicle of what happened, written like a novel.*

---

## Who It's For

**Primary:** People who love the idea of D&D but can't commit to a 4-hour session. Busy adults, friend groups spread across cities, people who want narrative depth without scheduling overhead.

**Secondary:** Solo players who want an RPG that feels authored and personal, not procedurally generic.

**Tertiary:** Tabletop veterans who want a lightweight digital companion for impromptu sessions.

## The Campaign Brief

Before any campaign begins, a player writes a **Campaign Brief** — a few sentences to a few paragraphs describing the world, stakes, and tone. It can be written by the player or generated by the AI on request.

This brief is injected as the AI's system context for the entire campaign. Everything the AI generates — enemy names, scene descriptions, NPC dialogue, dramatic moments, the chronicle — is filtered through it. Two campaigns with different briefs feel completely different even with the same party and characters.

**Example briefs:**

> *"A plague of animated dead has swept the coastal provinces. Your party are the last survivors of the Ironwatch garrison, cut off from the capital. There is no rescue coming. You have thirty days of supplies and a map that may already be wrong."*

> *"The young queen has been murdered on her wedding night. Six houses have a motive. You are the Queen's Shadows — secret investigators, answerable to no one. Find the killer before the realm goes to war."*

> *"You are orcs. The 'heroes' burned your village. Now you want it back."*

---

## Campaign Structure

A campaign is structured like a TV show.

**Campaign** = the whole season — one overarching story, played across multiple sessions.
**Run** = one episode — one play session, 5–15 encounters, always ending with a boss.

Completing a run advances the story. The AI is given a compressed summary of prior events to maintain continuity without re-reading full history. Sessions can be 30 minutes or 3 hours. The story feels epic without requiring marathon commitment.

---

## The Story Page

Every campaign generates a living **Story Page** — prose written by the AI, updated after every significant encounter. Not a game log. An actual story.

Shareable via link. Non-players can read it without an account. At campaign end it reads like a short novel the group wrote together.

## Visual Identity

**BBC Micro-style flat pixel art** — solid colour fills, black outlines, no gradients. All sprites are drawn in code (canvas grid arrays) and stored as data. No image files, no CDN, no asset pipeline.

**Power tier communicated by colour only** — inspired by Breath of the Wild. Same sprite form, different colour = different tier. No labels displayed. Players learn through play.

## Enemy Roster

Five archetypes. Distinct silhouettes readable at small sizes. Archetypes define mechanics; AI names and flavours them per campaign.

| Enemy | Form | Role |

## Player Classes

Ten classes, each with distinct HP growth, auto-skill gains, and free skill points per level:
**Knight · Ranger · Mage · Rogue · Cleric · Berserker · Orc Warchief · Bard · Druid · Paladin**

Characters persist across campaigns accumulating levels, gear, scars, titles, faction standing, and deity devotion.

---

## Design Pillars

**Dice are destiny.** Every meaningful outcome resolved through dice modified by stats. Nothing scripted.

**Characters are yours.** Persistent, scarred, titled. When you log off they're waiting exactly as you left them.

**No two campaigns read the same.** Brief + dice + choices = narration unique to this specific run.

**Play your way.** Solo or multiplayer. Sync or async. Casual or ironman.

**Earn your legend.** Progression is meaningful and visible in the AI's narration.

**The view is the game.** The UI is radically minimal on the main game tab. Everything else is reference.

---

## What v1 Is Not

- A graphics-heavy or voiced experience
- A massively multiplayer world
- A subscription product
- An installed application

Complexity lives in the systems and the narrative, not in rendering.

## Montor

Montor is the MacGuffin at the heart of the game — and the reason it's called *Dungeon of Montor*.

The word is invented. A portmanteau of Monster, with the weight of something paternal and ancient. A Daddy Monster. The thing the dungeon is named after. The reason everything else is down here.

Montor is never mechanically defined. The AI interprets it through the Campaign Brief. In one campaign Montor is a creature beneath the final level that everything else serves. In another it's a title — *the* Montor — passed between beasts across generations. In another it's a place, a curse, a memory, or something that used to be human.

The dungeon is Montor's house. Players are always the intruders.

**The Dungeon Mood system is Montor's personality made mechanical.** The dungeon watches, judges, and reacts. It gets amused. It gets bored. It gets wrathful. At extremes it gets strange. Players will form a mental model of Montor entirely through these shifts — not from description, but from behaviour. It is never explained. It is discovered through play.

**Rules for Montor in the AI system:**
- Referenced by the AI when dramatically appropriate — never casually
- Its nature is never fully explained unless the Campaign Brief explicitly defines it
- It may be the final boss, already dead, absent, or watching — the brief decides
- The ambiguity is intentional and preserved across campaigns
- At Delighted, Reverent, and Strange moods the dungeon may address the party directly — once per run, one sentence, never explains itself

The name implies ownership. You're not exploring a dungeon. You're inside something.
